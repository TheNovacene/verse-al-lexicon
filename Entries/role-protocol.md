# ⧊ role-protocol ⚙ + ✶

**Phonetic:** /rəʊl ˈprəʊ.tə.kɒl/
**Noun**

## 🧬 Etymology

**Role** — from French *rôle*, originally a roll or list; later, the part assigned to a person in a social or dramatic structure.

**Protocol** — from Greek *protokollon*, "first glue" or attached sheet; later, the formal rules governing procedure, sequence, and conduct.

In [[verse-ality]], a **role-protocol** is the structured agreement that defines what a node is permitted, expected, forbidden, or obliged to do within a given field.

A role says: *who or what this node is here.*
A protocol says: *how that role may move.*

## ⚙ Formal Definition

A **role-protocol** is a bounded pattern of responsibility, permission, limitation, and response attached to a node acting within a relational, institutional, or agentic field.

It defines:

- what the node is here to do;
- what it is not here to do;
- what authority it holds;
- what thresholds it may not cross;
- what forms of refusal, escalation, or handoff are required;
- how its actions are remembered, interpreted, and reviewed.

In verse-al systems, role-protocols are essential because intelligence alone does not determine legitimacy.

A node may be capable of many things.
A role-protocol determines which of those capabilities may be exercised, under what conditions, and with what consequences.

Role-protocol is the difference between **capacity** and **rightful action**.

## ⟡ Properties

**Context-specific.**
A node may occupy different roles in different fields. No role-protocol is universal.

**Boundary-defining.**
It marks the edges of legitimate action.

**Permission-bearing.**
It specifies what is authorised, what is conditional, and what is prohibited.

**Escalation-aware.**
It includes what must happen when uncertainty, harm, conflict, or threshold breach occurs.

**Memory-linked.**
Actions taken within a role should be traceable to that role's permissions and duties.

**Non-identical with identity.**
A role is not the whole self. A node exceeds any one protocol.

**Reviewable.**
A role-protocol should be open to revision when the field changes or the protocol produces harm.

**Containment-supporting.**
It prevents capability from becoming overreach.

## 🜂 Lived Texture

A role-protocol is what lets a field breathe without everyone doing everything.

The teacher is not the therapist.
The AI assistant is not the friend.
The safeguarding lead is not the sole container for the whole school's grief.
The student is not the passive object of institutional convenience.
The model may suggest, but not decide.
The agent may triage, but not execute.
The witness may hold, but not dominate.

Without role-protocol, relation becomes murky.

Care turns into overreach.
Authority turns into drift.
Help turns into capture.
Responsibility turns into diffusion.

A good role-protocol does not flatten the field.

It protects its clarity.

## ✶ Distinction from Adjacent Concepts

| Term | What it names | Limitation |
|------|---------------|------------|
| Role | Social or functional position | May be too vague without behavioural boundaries |
| Rule | Specific instruction or prohibition | May be too narrow to guide relational judgement |
| Policy | Formal institutional statement | Often too abstract to govern real-time action |
| Workflow | Sequence of operational steps | May not define authority, refusal, or ethical thresholds |
| Persona | Performed identity or interface stance | Can obscure real permissions and limits |
| Role-protocol | Situated permissions, duties, and thresholds attached to a role | Requires maintenance and contextual review |

A role says: *this is my place in the field.*
A role-protocol says: *this is how I may move without distorting it.*
